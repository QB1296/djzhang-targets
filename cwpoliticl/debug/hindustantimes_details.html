A bigot at the top: Boris Johnson is no joke; he is racist

  • Nic Dawes, Hindustan Times
    • | 
  • Updated: Jul 16, 2016 06:54 IST
British Foreign Secretary Boris Johnson reacts as he sings the French national anthem during a reception at the French Ambassador's residence in London on July 14. (AFP)

Every single bit of the internet that isn’t choked with Pokemon in this week of distressingly augmented reality, is full of Boris Johnson and his utter unsuitability to be Britain’s Foreign Secretary.

All of the hot-takes are right, he is an appalling choice, but all of them stop short. Why has no-one on the commanding heights of British journalism, or politics, been prepared to call Johnson what he is? An unblushing racist, whose talk of “piccaninnies”, and the glories of colonialism should have made him unemployable and unelectable long before 2008.

Instead we are reminded over and over in a hundred sniggering posts optimised for Facebook, that he is unsuitable for the job because he is “undiplomatic” or “insulting”.

The evidence for that case is now well rehearsed: Hilary Clinton looks “like a sadistic nurse in a mental hospital”, Turkey’s Recep Tayyip Erdogan is a “wankerer”, Africa’s problem is “not that we were once in charge, but that we are not in charge any more”. 

The Independent, in a mock-apology to the world said,“Johnson has insulted almost as many of you over the years as Prince Philip”; the FT offered “Boris Johnson: an undiplomatic history” , and the BBC “How Britain’s new foreign secretary has insulted the world”. 

The Economist’s Bagehot  captures perfectly the underlying sentiments of all of this coverage: scorn, embarrassment and anxiety about Britain’s prospects in the global community with Johnson at the FCO. And by coming closer than the others, he inadvertently highlights the reluctance of the British establishment to confront the real ugliness of Johnson’s worldview. 

“Personally he is likeable. But he is also gaffe-prone and the progenitor of a series of undiplomatic comments about other peoples. Much more damning: he is unscrupulous, unserious and poorly organised.”

Why is it not possible to say that by “other peoples” you mean “everyone who isn’t white” and that by “undiplomatic” you mean “evincing a conviction that they are shiftless, stupid, ungrateful cannibals”. And why is the gleeful bruiting of such convictions in the press much less damning than a lack of seriousness?

Consider Johnson’s February 2002 screed for the Spectator, “Cancel the Guilt Trip”, written ahead of a visit by Tony Blair to Nigeria, Ghana, Sierra Leone and Senegal. The thrust of the piece is that Africa’s problems are not “our” fault, but it goes much further than that.

Boris Johnson’s Africans aren’t just “piccaninnies” with “watermelon smiles”, they are clueless cartoon figures, agog at foreign dignitaries who arrive in their “big white birds” to deliver lessons in agricultural economics and nutrition.

“If left to their own devices, the natives [of Uganda] would rely on nothing but the instant carbohydrate gratification of the plantain. You never saw a place so abounding in bananas: great green barrel-sized bunches, off to be turned into matooke. Though this dish (basically fried banana) was greatly relished by Idi Amin, the colonists correctly saw that the export market was limited”.

If that isn’t enough, there is more. White people according to Johnson, came to Africa to rescue black people from brown people.

“ Are we guilty of slavery? Pshaw. It was one of the first duties of Frederick Lugard, who colonised Buganda in the 1890s, to take on and defeat the Arab slavers”. 

Britain’s foundational role in the transatlantic trade, and all of its enduring horrors, the 46,000 British slave owners whose compensation in 1834 for their loss of “property” at following abolition would consume some 40% of all state expenditure are, of course, completely elided .

The Speccy is a small and nasty magazine, and 2002 was long ago, but there is fresher evidence. Writing this year in the large and influential Sun, Johnson rebuked Barack Obama for his views on Brexit by way of a parable about the removal from the Oval Office of bust of Churchill. “Some said it was a symbol of the part-Kenyan President’s ancestral dislike of the British empire – of which Churchill had been such a fervent defender”.

In BoJo’s birther fantasy, it is the racial memory of a not-quite-American black president that motivates his views on Britain’s place in Europe. Compacted into the same short sentence is an implied defence of Churchill’s imperialism, and Britain’s conduct during its battle to maintain control of Kenya. Both, of course, are indefensible.

It emerged in 2012 that many of the records covering British conduct during the Mau Mau insurgency had been destroyed on the orders of officials in the department Johnson now heads to avoid “embarrassment”. Those that remain record widespread torture and murder by colonial security forces. 

In “Britain’s gulag”, historian Caroline Elkins records the brutal tactics employed under Churchill to suppress Kenya’s struggle against colonialism. "Electric shock was widely used, as well as cigarettes and fire," she writes. "The screening teams whipped, shot, burned, and mutilated Mau Mau suspects.”

Members of the Obama family have told researchers that the President’s grandfather, Hussein Onyango Obama, was one of those detained and severely tortured during this period. Ancestral dislike indeed.

When Johnson tosses off quips about piccanninies and cannibals, when he reduces Africans to grinning children, and when he calls for a new imperialism, he is not being undiplomatic. These are not gaffes. They are the revealed structure of a racist worldview.

Britain’s media and political class are stunned and embarrassed that a rude, unserious, conniving man has become their chief representative to the world. The truth is that they made him possible.

I am a white South African. I know a thing or two about the social processes that accommodate and sustain racism: the dinner guests who keep chewing, uncomfortable perhaps, but silent, as some casual slur clunks onto the table, the talk of “standards” in university admissions and employment that covers for structural prejudice, and above it all, the pained, resentful, howl, “its not our fault, apartheid ended 20 years ago, get over it”.

When he slipped easily into political life to as a candidate for Mayor of London, one of the world's most diverse cities, criticism focussed on his posh antecedents and sometimes on his buffoonery, almost never on his nostalgia for the globalised racism of empire. If Brexit has brought out into the streets some of the viler xenophobic undercurrents of British national life, then Johnson is the grinning mascot for establishment bigotry.

Editors, proprietors, fellow journalists and British voters who paid him, or gave him a pass, made Boris Johnson possible. Their anger now, and shame, is the rage of Caliban seeing his face in glass. Perhaps that is a start.
